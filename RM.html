01

Introduction to IPR

Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) are legal rights given to persons over the creations of their minds. They usually give the creator an exclusive right over the use of his/her creation for a certain period of time.

"The importance of IPR was first recognized in the Paris Convention (1883) and the Berne Convention (1886)."

03

Patentability Criteria

1

Novelty

The invention must be new and not part of the existing "prior art" anywhere in the world.

2

Inventive Step

It must not be obvious to a person with average knowledge in that specific technical field.

3

Industrial Application

The invention must be capable of being made or used in some kind of industry.

Non-Patentable Subject Matter

Scientific theories, mathematical methods, plant or animal varieties, and mere discoveries of natural substances are generally excluded from patentability.

Software Protection

In most jurisdictions, software code is protected as a "Literary Work" under copyright law. This protects the specific code written but not the underlying algorithm or functionality.

IPR in IT & Software

The Dual Protection Dilemma

  • COPYRIGHT Protects source code as "literary work". Primary mode of global protection.
  • PATENTS Software per se is not patentable (Sec 3k). Must show "technical effect" or hardware integration.
