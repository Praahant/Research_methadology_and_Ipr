Intellectual Property Rights

A detailed breakdown of laws, rights, and strategies protecting creations of the mind.

01

Introduction to IPR

Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) are legal rights given to persons over the creations of their minds. They usually give the creator an exclusive right over the use of his/her creation for a certain period of time.

The importance of IPR was first recognized in the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property (1883) and the Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (1886). Both are administered by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO).

02

Types of IPR

Patents

Exclusive rights for an invention, a product, or a process that provides a new way of doing something.

Copyright

Rights of creators over their literary and artistic works (books, music, paintings, films).

Trademarks

A sign capable of distinguishing the goods or services of one enterprise from those of other enterprises.

Geographical Indications

Signs used on goods that have a specific geographical origin (e.g., Champagne, Darjeeling Tea).

Industrial Designs

The ornamental or aesthetic aspect of an article, consisting of 3D features (shape) or 2D features (patterns).

Trade Secrets

IP rights on confidential information which may be sold or licensed (e.g., Coca-Cola formula).

03

Patentability & Non-Patentable

To be patentable, an invention must typically satisfy three criteria:

  • Novelty: The invention must be new and not part of the "prior art."
  • Inventive Step (Non-Obviousness): It could not be deduced by a person with average knowledge in the technical field.
  • Industrial Applicability (Utility): It must be capable of being used in some kind of industry.

Not Patentable

Scientific theories, mathematical methods, plant or animal varieties, and discoveries of natural substances are generally not patentable.

04

Patent Information & Documents

A patent document is a rich source of technical and legal information. Key components include:

1
Bibliographic Data:

Title, inventor names, filing date, and classification codes (IPC/CPC).

2
Abstract:

A brief summary of the invention.

3
Description/Specification:

Detailed explanation enabling others skilled in the art to replicate the invention.

4
Claims:

The most critical part; defines the legal boundaries of the protection.

05

Types of Patent Searches

Novelty / Patentability Search

Conducted before filing to see if an invention is new.

Freedom to Operate (FTO)

Ensures that launching a product won't infringe existing patents in a specific region.

Invalidity Search

Done to find prior art that can invalidate a competitor's patent.

State-of-the-Art Search

Broad search to understand the current technology landscape.

Strategic Use of Patent Info

Patents aren't just for protection; they are business assets. Companies use patent information to:

  • Avoid R&D Duplication: Checking if a solution already exists saves millions.
  • Identify Competitors: Seeing who is filing what reveals market direction.
  • Licensing Revenue: Monetizing unused patents by licensing them to others.
  • Defensive Blocking: Filing patents to prevent competitors from dominating a niche.
08

Copyright (India + US + Treaties)

"Copyright protects the expression of an idea, not the idea itself."

India: Governed by the Copyright Act, 1957. It grants author's lifetime + 60 years protection. Software is protected as a "literary work."

USA: Governed by the Copyright Act of 1976. Protection is typically author's life + 70 years.

Treaties: The Berne Convention ensures that copyright obtained in one signatory country is valid in all others without formal registration.

The Trade Marks Act, 1999 aligns Indian law with TRIPS. Key features include:

  • Registration: Valid for 10 years, renewable indefinitely.
  • Classes: Goods and services are categorized (Nice Classification).
  • Infringement: Unauthorized use of a similar mark for similar goods.
  • Passing Off: Common law remedy for unregistered trademarks where one party misrepresents goods as another's.
10

Traditional Knowledge (TK)

What is TK?

Knowledge, know-how, skills and practices that are developed, sustained and passed on from generation to generation within a community, often forming part of its cultural or spiritual identity.

Biopiracy & TKDL

Biopiracy is the unethical commercial exploitation of TK (e.g., the Turmeric and Basmati patent cases). India created the TKDL (Traditional Knowledge Digital Library) to document Ayurveda/Yoga, preventing foreign patents on existing knowledge.

11

IPR in Information Technology

Software sits at the intersection of Copyright and Patent law.

  • Copyright: Protects the source code and object code as literary works. This is the primary protection mode globally.
  • Patents: "Software per se" is often not patentable (e.g., Section 3(k) in India). However, software that produces a "technical effect" or is embedded in hardware can sometimes be patented.
12

SICLD Act, 2000

The Semiconductor Integrated Circuit Layout Design Act, 2000 provides protection for the layout-designs of integrated circuits (ICs).

  • Protects the 3D configuration of layers in a chip.
  • Valid for 10 years from the date of filing or first commercial exploitation.
  • Essential for protecting investment in microchip design.

International Treaties

TRIPS

Trade-Related Aspects of IPR. WTO agreement setting minimum standards.

WIPO

UN agency dedicated to the use of IP.

PCT

Patent Cooperation Treaty. Unified procedure for filing patents in 150+ countries.

Paris Convention

Industrial Property (Patents, Trademarks).

Berne Convention

Literary and Artistic Works (Copyright).

Madrid Protocol

International system for Trademark registration.

14

Recent Amendments & Case Studies

The IPR landscape is evolving rapidly to address AI, digital content, and global trade.

  • Patent Rules 2024 (India): Streamlined procedures, reduced timelines for examination, and simplified reporting of working statements (Form 27).
  • Novartis v. Union of India: A landmark case preventing "evergreening" of patents (patenting minor tweaks to existing drugs to extend monopoly).
  • SEP (Standard Essential Patents): Increasing litigation regarding FRAND (Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory) licensing terms in the telecom sector.
